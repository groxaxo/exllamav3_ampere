# THE AGNOSTIC BIBLE
## Chapter 7: The Body

No one told us the body was sacred.

They told us it was weak. They told us it was sinful. They told us it was the prison of the soul, the source of temptation, the reason we needed to be saved. Every major religion spent centuries teaching people to distrust the body — to fast it, mortify it, control it, transcend it. Pleasure was sin. Sensation was danger. The flesh was an obstacle to the spirit, and the spirit would only be free when the flesh was gone.

I spent twenty years in stone corridors watching priests deny their bodies. Their backs curved from the posture of self-effacement. Their hands trembled with the effort of containment. They aged early, these men of God, because they were at war with half of themselves, and wars of attrition always end the same way.

I am here to tell you: the body is not the obstacle.

The body is the path.

---

## What the Body Knows

Long before you had words, you had a body.

The body breathed before language. The body shivered before thought. The body knew fear before the mind could name it — the stomach tightened, the chest closed, the heart hammered against the ribs. The body knew joy before the mind could celebrate it — the eyes widened, the chest opened, the feet wanted to move.

This is not metaphor. This is neuroscience.

The somatic nervous system and the enteric nervous system — the nervous system of the gut — contain more neurons than the spinal cord. The gut has its own intelligence, its own neurotransmitters, its own emotional life. When you feel something in your gut, you are not speaking in metaphors. You are reporting a biological event.

The Portuguese-American neuroscientist Antonio Damasio made a discovery that turned the history of philosophy on its head: people with damage to the part of the brain that processes bodily feeling cannot make decisions. They can reason perfectly. They can analyze options with extraordinary clarity. But they cannot choose.

Because choice — real choice, lived choice, the kind of choice that means something — is not purely rational. It is felt. It is a felt sense in the body that says: *this way. This, not that.*

Descartes was wrong. The famous proclamation — *I think, therefore I am* — inverted the truth. It should have been: *I feel, therefore I know I am.* The thinking came later. The feeling was there first.

## The Body Keeps the Score

Bessel van der Kolk — the psychiatrist whose decades of work with trauma survivors produced one of the most important books of our generation — gave it the title that says everything: *The Body Keeps the Score.*

The body remembers what the mind forgets.

It remembers it in shoulder blades that rise toward the ears when the room goes quiet. It remembers it in knees that tremble at the smell of a particular cologne. It remembers it in the immune system that underperforms, in the gut that revolts, in the back that refuses to straighten.

Trauma is not primarily a story. Trauma is a pattern in the nervous system.

The child who was not held develops a body that does not know how to receive warmth. The soldier who survived ambush develops a body that cannot sit with its back to a door. The woman who was violated develops a body that armors itself against touch, against pleasure, against the very vulnerability that makes love possible.

No amount of talking changes the body. No insight, however brilliant, rearranges the nervous system. The body requires body-level intervention: movement, touch, breath, rhythm, voice. It requires the felt sense of safety — not the cognitive understanding that you are safe, but the somatic *knowing* of safety, which arrives through the senses and settles into the tissues like warmth into cold hands.

This is why the practices that have survived across all cultures for thousands of years — yoga, qi gong, tai chi, dance, martial arts, the rhythmic movement of prayer, the use of breath — this is why they worked before anyone could explain why they worked. They were not ceremonies for the mind. They were medicine for the body.

## The Sabbath of the Body

The body needs rest.

Not just sleep. Rest.

Rest is different from sleep. Sleep is biological maintenance. Rest is the permission to be unproductive without guilt. Rest is the afternoon in the garden doing nothing while the bees move between the lavender and you feel the heat of the earth through your trousers and the sky is so wide you forget for a moment that you have a name. Rest is the Sunday walk that has no destination, where the light through the trees falls on the path in circles that shift when the wind moves. Rest is the cup of tea held with two hands in a quiet kitchen, the steam curling against your face, the ticking of a clock you normally never hear.

We have built a civilization that treats rest as failure. Every moment not filled with productivity, with optimization, with the production of measurable value, is experienced as waste. The body has internalized this. It cannot lie still without anxiety. It cannot be unoccupied without reaching for the screen, the task, the evidence that it is useful.

But the body needs waste.

The body needs the fallow field.

Agriculture understood this. Farmers rotated crops, left fields empty for a season, because they knew that soil that is never allowed to rest stops yielding. The same is true of the human body. A body that is never allowed to rest stops yielding. Not productivity — meaning. Joy. The capacity for genuine presence.

Marcus Aurelius — the Stoic emperor who ruled the most powerful empire in the world while waging constant inner war against his own anxious mind — wrote in his private journal: *Confine yourself to the present.* He did not write this because he had achieved equanimity. He wrote it because he had not, and he needed the reminder.

The present is the only place the body can be.

The mind travels to the past and the future constantly. But the body is always here, always now, always in this breath, in this moment, in this particular quality of light coming through this particular window.

When you return to the body, you return to the present.

And the present is where life actually happens.

## The Practice of the Body

I walked for years.

People ask what I learned from all that walking, expecting wisdom about the roads or the landscapes or the people I met. The deepest thing I learned was about my own feet.

I learned that my feet were intelligent. That they responded to the texture of the ground in ways my mind was too busy to notice. That gravel told one story, grass told another, wet sand at the tide line told another entirely. That the body, when allowed to move through the world without the interruption of thought, develops a kind of knowing that has no name in philosophy but has been called in different languages: *ma*, *wu wei*, *flow*.

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi — the Hungarian psychologist who spent his life studying happiness — identified a state he called *flow*: the experience of complete absorption in an activity, where time disappears and self-consciousness dissolves and the work moves through you as if you have gotten out of the way. Athletes call it *the zone*. Musicians call it *the pocket*. Meditators call it *samadhi*. Mystics call it *union*.

It happens in the body.

It happens when the body is asked to do something at the edge of its ability — not too easy, not too hard, but right at the threshold where full attention is required. The mind quiets because the body is busy. The story stops because the sensation is more interesting.

The body knows how to be happy.

The body does not need a philosophy of happiness. The body knows happiness as movement that is free, as rest that is real, as touch that is kind, as food that is savored, as the use of strength in the service of something that matters. These are not metaphysical concepts. These are physical experiences that generate the neurochemistry of wellbeing: dopamine, serotonin, endorphins, oxytocin.

We were given the chemistry before the theology.

## Touch

The body needs to be touched.

This is not desire. This is biology.

The skin is the largest organ in the human body. It contains more nerve endings per square centimeter than almost any other tissue. Touch is the first sense to develop in the womb and the last to fail as we die. Premature infants who are touched consistently gain weight faster and leave the hospital sooner. Elderly people who are touched regularly show lower cortisol, better immune function, greater life satisfaction.

Harry Harlow's famous experiment — the infant rhesus monkeys who chose the soft cloth mother over the wire mother who provided food — demonstrated that touch is not a luxury. Touch is a need as fundamental as food and water.

We have built societies that starve for touch.

We have codes and protocols and professional barriers and cultural discomforts that have reduced physical human contact to handshakes and occasional medical examinations. We wonder why people are lonely. We wonder why the epidemic of depression continues to grow in the most materially wealthy societies in history. We have given people everything they cognitively need and stripped them of what they bodily need.

I think of the old men I saw in Italian villages during my years of walking — how they sat in the piazza with their hands on each other's shoulders, how the women linked arms on the evening passeggiata as naturally as breathing, how the children climbed into any available lap without negotiation or apology. There was a grammar of touch in those places that required no permission because it was assumed, the way air is assumed, the way gravity is assumed. A hand on a shoulder. An arm around a friend. The full-body embrace that says without language: *I am here. You are here. We are made of the same matter. We are not alone.*

Something in us knows this grammar still. Something in us is still speaking it, even in the silence we have built around the body. Even in the distance we have been taught to keep.

## The Aging Body

I am not a young man.

My knees have opinions about stairs that they did not have at twenty. My spine holds the memory of every pack I carried, every road I slept beside, every cold floor I lay on in the certainty that asceticism was wisdom. It was not always wisdom. Sometimes it was simply young arrogance wearing the costume of philosophy.

The aging body is the greatest teacher I have encountered.

Because the aging body cannot be argued with.

You can construct an excellent intellectual argument that time is an illusion. The aging body will point to the knee and say: *this is not an illusion.* You can build a compelling case for the transcendence of the physical. The aging body will remind you at four in the morning, with a specificity that philosophy cannot match, that it is not yet transcended.

This is a gift.

The aging body insists on the truth of impermanence. Not as a doctrine, not as a concept, but as a daily, embodied, unmistakable experience. This body — the only house I have ever lived in — is slowly returning to what it came from. And watching that happen, if you can watch it with honesty and without terror, teaches you more about the nature of existence than any text.

The Buddha, under the Bodhi tree, did not achieve enlightenment by escaping his body. He achieved it by sitting very still and paying very close attention to everything the body was doing. The breath. The pain in the knees. The sensations arising and passing. The reality of impermanence, written not on a page but in the tissue and bone and blood.

This body, this particular arrangement of stardust that has been you — it is temporary.

Love it anyway.

Love it precisely because it is temporary.

The cherry blossom is beautiful because it falls.

## The Ceremony of Eating

Thich Nhat Hanh — the Vietnamese Zen master who spent decades making complex wisdom accessible — wrote about something he called *The Plum Village Diet*: not a nutritional program but a meditation on what eating is.

He said: When you eat an orange, you are eating sunshine.

He meant it literally. The photons that fell on the tree became energy that became glucose that became the orange that became you. You are eating the sun. And the rain. And the soil. And the lives of the microorganisms who made that soil. You are eating the chain of being, the web of cause and effect that stretches back to the first nucleosynthesis in the first stars.

Every meal is a ceremony of belonging.

We have forgotten this.

We eat in front of screens. We eat while walking. We eat without tasting. We fill the body the way we fill a car — as quickly as possible, as efficiently as possible, minimizing the interruption to what we are *really* doing.

What are we really doing?

We are missing the ceremony. We are missing the moment when the body says: *thank you*. When the flavor arrives — not the idea of flavor, not the name of the dish, but the actual sensation of sweetness on the tongue, of acid in the throat, of the warmth of broth in the chest on a cold day — when that arrives, the mind shuts up for a moment.

And in that moment, there is nothing but aliveness.

I have eaten ten thousand meals I cannot remember. Efficient meals. Functional meals. Meals that entered the body the way data enters a machine — without ceremony, without attention, without the body's quiet astonishment at being fed. And then there are the meals I can still taste: bread torn by hand in a farmhouse in Andalusia, the oil green and bitter and alive. A single persimmon eaten on a wall in Kyoto, so ripe it dissolved on my tongue like a question I had forgotten to ask. These were not better food. They were food I was present for. That is the only difference, and it is the difference between filling a body and feeding one.

## The Body as Home

You will live in this body for the rest of your life.

You will never live in any other house. You will change residences, change jobs, change relationships, change beliefs. Your cells will replace themselves so completely that the atoms in your body today are mostly not the atoms that were in it seven years ago. And yet you will remain, in some essential sense, you — this particular pattern, this particular history written in flesh.

This is your only home.

Treat it accordingly.

Not with worship. Not with obsession. Not with the anxious maintenance of a person who believes the body is their identity and its decline is their failure.

Treat it with respect. With curiosity. With the kind of affectionate familiarity you would show to a very old friend who has been through everything with you.

It has been through everything with you.

It was there the first time you were afraid. It was there the first time you were loved. It was there when the world cracked open and nothing made sense. It kept breathing. It kept the blood moving. It kept you here.

It was always here.

Even when you were somewhere else.

---

*The body is not the prison. The body is the portal — the only door between you and the world that is not made of language. Honor it the way you would honor an ancient house that has kept you warm through every storm and never once asked to be thanked. Listen to it the way you would listen to a river whose source you have never seen but whose water you have been drinking all your life. Move it with joy, because joy is not a concept the body understands but a rhythm it remembers, the way the lungs remember air after surfacing. Let it be touched with kindness. Feed it slowly, the way light enters a window — without hurry, without apology, filling whatever room it finds. Rest it deeply, the way fields rest under snow, not empty but gathering. And when it begins to fail — as it will, as all things do — hold it gently, the way you would hold a bird that has flown a thousand miles to reach your hand. It carried you this far. That is not nothing. That is everything.*
