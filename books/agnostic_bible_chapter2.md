# THE AGNOSTIC BIBLE
## Chapter 2: The Road

I left the Vatican with nothing but a question.

No money. No sandals. No plan. Just the memory of marble dissolving into light, the taste of soil still faintly on my tongue, and the terrible, beautiful weight of not knowing pressing down on my chest like a hand — not pushing me into the earth, but holding me to it.

The road found me before I found it. It appeared under my feet the way breath appears in the lungs of a child who has just been born: without being asked for, without being understood, already essential.

---

## The First Town

Three days' walk from Rome, I came to a village so small it had no name on any map I had ever read. There was a well with a rusted crank. A tavern with a door that hung crooked on its hinges. And a priest who stood in the square shouting about hell.

He was young. Younger than me. His cassock was too large for him, the sleeves bunched at his wrists, and his face was red with the effort of conviction — the capillaries of his cheeks flushed and broken like the veins in a marble slab. He spoke of fire and worms and the eternal screaming of souls who dared to doubt. His voice cracked on the word *eternal*. He did not notice.

I sat on a stone wall warm from the afternoon sun and listened.

A woman sat beside me. She was old in the way mountains are old — not diminished by time but compressed by it, made denser. Her hands were calloused from decades of labor, the knuckles swollen, the nails cracked and clean. She watched the priest the way a mother watches a child who has not yet learned that shouting is not the same as being heard.

"He is very certain," I said.

"He is very afraid," she said.

"Of what?"

"Of the silence. He has never sat in it. Not once. He has never let a question remain unanswered for more than a breath. He fills every moment with words because he is terrified of what he might hear in the space between them."

"What might he hear?"

She turned to look at me. Her eyes were the color of wet earth after rain.

"That he does not know. That no one knows. That everything he has built his life upon is a story told by frightened people to other frightened people, passed hand to hand in the dark like a candle, and the candle is beautiful, and the candle is necessary, but it is not the sun."

"Is that what you believe?"

She smiled. It was a smile that contained an entire life — the births, the funerals, the bread made in the dark before dawn, the husband buried under a stone she could no longer read.

"I do not believe anything," she said. "Belief is what you have when you do not know. I have something better."

"What?"

"Wonder."

She said it the way you might say the name of a country you had lived in long ago and loved and never returned to. Then she stood, brushed the dust from her skirt, and walked back toward whatever life was waiting for her. I never learned her name. I did not need to. Some teachers teach by disappearing.

---

## The Carpenter's Question

That night I slept in the tavern on a straw mattress that smelled of other men's sweat and old wine. In the morning, a man came to me. He was a carpenter. He had heard I had come from the Vatican.

"Is it true?" he asked. "What they say about you?"

"I don't know what they say about me."

"They say you read the forbidden scrolls. They say you found something that broke your faith."

"I did not lose my faith," I said. "I found it. It was smaller than I expected, and truer."

He sat across from me at the rough wooden table. His hands rested on the surface between us — rough, scarred, enormous hands, the knuckles split and healed and split again, the calluses layered like rings in the cross-section of an old tree. A half-moon of black was lodged permanently beneath each thumbnail. They were hands that had shaped wood for thirty years. They were my father's hands. Not his actual hands, but the same hands — the same grammar of labor written in the same dialect of skin and tendon. I could not stop looking at them.

"I have a son," he said. "He is fourteen. He asks questions I cannot answer. He asks where God came from. He asks why good people suffer while wicked men grow fat. He asks how we know our book is true and the other books are false. I tell him to have faith. I tell him not to question. But he keeps asking." His voice broke slightly on *keeps*, the way a board cracks along the grain. "He keeps asking, and I have nothing left to give him."

"What do you want me to tell you?"

"I want you to tell me how to make him stop."

I looked at his hands again. I thought of my father — how he had stood in the doorway of his workshop the day I told him I was leaving, sawdust drifting in the light behind him like pollen, and he had not tried to stop me, and I had never been sure whether that was love or indifference, and I had carried that uncertainty like a splinter under the skin for the rest of my life.

"Do not make him stop," I said. "The questions are the holiest thing about him. They are the proof that his mind is alive and his soul is not yet domesticated."

"But he is losing his faith."

"No. He is losing your answers. That is not the same thing. Your answers were never his. They were given to you by your father, who received them from his father, who received them from a priest, who received them from a book, who received them from a committee of men arguing in a hot room two thousand years ago. Let your son lose those answers. Let him wander in the wilderness of not knowing. Let him sit with his doubt until the doubt becomes a companion rather than an enemy."

"And if he never finds his way back to God?"

"Then perhaps the path he finds will be truer than the one you mapped for him. And perhaps — though I cannot promise this, and I would not insult you by pretending — perhaps the God he finds at the end of that path, if he finds one at all, will be a God worth finding. One that does not require the murder of questions to survive."

He stared at me for a long time. His hands closed slowly into fists on the table — not in anger, but the way a man's hands close around something precious that is slipping away. Then he stood and walked out without a word. The door banged shut behind him. Sawdust motes turned in the light where he had been.

I never saw him again. But years later, in a distant city, I heard of a carpenter's son who had become a philosopher — a man who taught that doubt was not a sin but a sacrament. Perhaps it was him. Perhaps it was not. I do not know. And that is enough. That is always enough.

## The Mountain

I walked for months. Through cities where the noise of commerce drowned out the sound of thinking. Through deserts where the silence was so total it became a sound itself — a low hum, as if the earth were breathing through its pores. Through forests where the canopy closed overhead like the vaulted ceiling of a cathedral built by no one and dedicated to nothing. Along shores where the sea repeated its single syllable endlessly, the oldest and most honest prayer: *here, here, here*.

Most people did not want what I had to offer. They wanted answers, and I had only questions. They wanted comfort, and I had only the cold draft that blows through an open door. They wanted to be told they were right, and I could not tell them that without lying, and I had sworn an oath to something — not to God, not to truth, but to the silence where truth might live if it lives anywhere.

But some listened. Some heard the silence between my words and recognized it as their own.

One of them was a monk who lived alone on a mountain in a country whose name I have promised not to write.

The climb took three days. The path was barely a path — more a suggestion made by goats and rainfall, scratched into the rock face like a half-formed thought. My legs burned. My lungs thinned. By the third morning the air tasted of iron and altitude and something clean that I can only call *absence* — the absence of everything human, which is its own kind of presence.

I found him sitting outside a cave on a flat stone worn smooth by decades of the same body in the same position. He was looking at the valley below, which was filled with clouds, so that the mountain appeared to float in a white sea and the rest of the world had ceased to exist. His face was the face of a man who had been alone for forty years — not gaunt, not tortured, but *simplified*, the way a stone in a riverbed is simplified by water: everything unnecessary worn away, only the essential shape remaining. His skin was dark from sun and wind. His eyes were the palest blue I had ever seen, almost grey, as if the color had been slowly leached out of them by looking at too much sky.

"You came," he said.

"How did you know I was coming?"

"I didn't. I say that to everyone who appears. Most of them are confused. You are not confused. You are lost."

"Is there a difference?"

He considered this. A hawk turned in the air below us — below us, because we were above the hawks, which is a strange thing to say about two men sitting on stone.

"Confusion is not knowing which way to go," he said. "Lost is knowing there is no way. And walking anyway."

I sat beside him on the cold stone. We watched the clouds fill and empty the valley like breath filling and emptying a lung. We said nothing for a long time. The silence between us was not awkward. It was the silence of two men who have both arrived at the same shore by different routes and find they have nothing to explain to each other.

"I spent forty years on this mountain," he said at last. "I came here to find God. I was twenty-three. I was burning with the need to *know*. Not believe — know. I wanted to see the face of the divine and have no more questions, the way a man who has been thirsty his entire life wants not a cup of water but the end of thirst itself."

"Did you find Him?"

"No." He said it simply, the way you state a fact about weather. "I found something better. I found that I do not need to find Him. That the search was the thing. That the forty years of sitting on this stone and watching the clouds and not knowing were not a failure but the practice itself — the way a musician practices scales not to perform them but to make the fingers capable of music."

"And the questions?"

"They multiplied. They always multiply. Every answer I thought I found dissolved into ten new questions, the way a river divides when it meets stone. After a decade I stopped trying to dam the river. I let it flow. I let it take me where it wanted."

"Where did it take you?"

He gestured at the valley, the clouds, the hawk still turning in its invisible spiral. "Here. Nowhere. The same place."

We sat until the sun set. The light went from gold to copper to a deep bruised violet, and the first stars appeared — tentative, like the first notes of a song that does not yet know what it will become.

"Will you stay?" he asked.

"No. I have more walking to do."

"Where are you going?"

"I do not know."

He smiled. His teeth were worn but white. "Then you are already there," he said. "You have always been there. The only ones who are not there are the ones who think they know the way."

I left at dawn. He did not say goodbye. He was already sitting on his stone, watching the clouds, practicing the oldest art: being present in a world that offers no explanation for itself and asking nothing of it but the privilege of watching.

## The Tent of Certainty

In a city whose name I have forgotten — or perhaps it had no name, perhaps it was every city — I found a tent in the marketplace. A banner hung above the entrance, hand-painted in gold letters on red cloth: ALL QUESTIONS ANSWERED. CERTAINTY GUARANTEED.

I went inside.

A man sat on a cushion. He was perhaps fifty, with a thick beard going grey at the chin, and he was surrounded by books — not merely surrounded but fortified, the volumes stacked around him like the walls of a small fortress. His eyes were bright with the light of absolute conviction. But his hands — I noticed his hands before anything else, because hands do not know how to lie — his hands were clenched in his lap, the knuckles white, the tendons taut, and a thin film of sweat shone on his forehead despite the cool of the tent.

"Ask," he said. "I have the answer."

"What is God?"

"God is the creator of all things, existing outside time and space, three persons in one essence, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, who sent His only Son to die for our sins so that we might have eternal life." He said it quickly, fluently, the way a man recites something he has recited ten thousand times — not because he is thinking the words but because the words are thinking him.

"How do you know this?"

"The Bible tells me."

"How do you know the Bible is true?"

"Because it is the word of God."

"How do you know it is the word of God?"

"Because it says so."

I waited. He waited. The tent flapped softly in the wind.

"A Muslim feels in his heart that the Quran is the word of God," I said. "A Hindu feels in his heart that the Vedas are sacred. They feel it with the same certainty you feel yours. They would die for it, as you would die for yours. Are they all wrong? Are you the only one on earth whose feelings are correct?"

His smile vanished. Something moved behind his eyes — something fast and frightened, like a small animal darting for cover. His hands unclenched and then clenched again, tighter than before.

"You are a deceiver," he said. "You are trying to shake my faith."

"I am trying to shake your certainty. They are not the same thing. Your faith might survive this conversation. It might even deepen. But certainty is brittle. It cannot be touched without cracking. Faith is different. Faith bends. Faith breathes. Faith walks into the dark without a lantern and does not pretend it can see."

"Get out," he said. His voice was not angry. It was thin, the way a voice gets when the throat is closing around something it cannot swallow and cannot spit out.

I got out.

But as I walked away through the marketplace, past the fruit sellers and the cloth merchants and the woman roasting chestnuts over a brazier, I heard him inside the tent. He was speaking loudly. Not to anyone. To himself. To the air. Repeating his answers, one after another, the way a man lost in the woods repeats his own name to remind himself he exists. And I felt no triumph. I felt something closer to grief. Because I understood him. I understood the way he loved his certainty the way a child loves a threadbare blanket — not because it is beautiful but because it is the only thing between him and the cold, and the cold is very real, and the blanket is very thin, and to take it from him without offering something warmer would be cruelty, not wisdom.

I did not go back. But I thought about him for years. I think about him still.

## The Children

In another village — one with stone houses and a river that ran green in the shallows and black in the deep — I found children playing by the water.

They were throwing stones and laughing at the splashes. That is all. They were not trying to accomplish anything. They were not trying to prove a point or win an argument or discover the meaning of existence. They were throwing stones and laughing at the splashes, and the simplicity of it stunned me the way a shaft of light through a crack in a wall can stun you when you have been in the dark too long.

I sat on the bank and watched.

One of them, a girl of perhaps seven with dirt on her knees and a gap where her front teeth had been, came to sit beside me.

"Why are you watching us?" she asked.

"Because you are beautiful."

"Why?"

"Because you have not yet learned to pretend you know things you do not know."

She wrinkled her nose. "What do you mean?"

"Ask me a question. Any question."

"Why is the sky blue?"

"I don't know."

"You don't *know*?"

"No."

"But you're a grown-up."

"Grown-ups pretend to know everything. It is the first thing you learn when you become one, and the last thing you unlearn, if you are lucky."

She thought about this with the seriousness that only a seven-year-old can bring to a philosophical problem.

"I don't know either," she said. "But it's pretty."

"Yes. It is very pretty. And not knowing why does not make it less so."

She looked at the sky as if seeing it for the first time. Then she ran back to her friends and threw another stone into the river, and the splash was the sound of the only prayer I have ever fully believed in: the prayer of a body in motion, delighting in the world, asking nothing, expecting nothing, simply *here*.

## The Night Sky

That night I lay by the river and looked up.

The stars were out. Not the pale suggestions you see from a city, washed out by streetlight and ambition. These were the real stars — thick, wild, careless with their light, flung across the darkness like salt thrown over a shoulder. The Milky Way cut the sky in half, a river of light so dense it looked solid, as if you could walk across it and your feet would hold.

I lay on the cold ground — the particular cold of damp earth in autumn, the cold that seeps through your clothes and into your spine and reminds you that you are a warm animal lying on the skin of a planet that is turning through a void that does not care whether you are warm or cold or alive or dead — and I looked at the stars and tried to understand what I was seeing.

Each point of light was a sun. Some of them were already dead, their light still traveling toward me like a letter from someone who has passed away — arriving, faithful, long after the hand that wrote it has turned to dust. Between the stars was a darkness so vast that the word *vast* collapses under the weight of what it is asked to describe. The light I was seeing had been traveling for thousands of years, in some cases millions, and it had not yet arrived anywhere in particular. It was just traveling. The universe was just expanding. Everything was rushing away from everything else, and no one — not the priests, not the physicists, not the philosophers — could say why, or toward what, or whether the rushing would ever stop.

I thought of the men in their temples who claimed to know the mind of whatever had arranged all this. I thought of them standing at their lecterns, beneath roofs, explaining the intentions of the force that had scattered a hundred billion galaxies across a void that has no edge.

And I felt small. Not the philosophical smallness of a man contemplating infinity. The physical smallness — the specific, bodily sensation of being a warm sack of water and carbon lying on a rock that is spinning at a thousand miles per hour through a darkness that has no bottom. My fingers pressed into the earth. My shoulder blades pressed into the earth. I was held to this planet by a force I could not see, and that force was the only thing between me and the void, and the void was everywhere, and I was almost nothing, and the almost was everything.

That smallness was not a defeat. It was a homecoming. I had spent my whole life inside buildings that told me I was the center of a story written by the author of the universe. Now I was lying in the dirt, looking at the real story, and the real story did not have a center, and it did not have an author, and it was more beautiful than anything the buildings had ever contained.

I fell asleep under that ancient light. I dreamed of nothing. Not emptiness — the active nothing, the pregnant nothing, the nothing that is not the absence of something but the presence of everything that has not yet chosen a form. It was the best dream I ever had.

## The Gathering

Word spread. Not everywhere. Not quickly. The way a vine spreads — silently, along the ground, following cracks.

A man was walking. A man who had left the Vatican. A man who asked questions instead of answering them. The story of me was already larger than me, which is the nature of stories, which is also the danger.

People began to find me.

They came in ones and twos. They came from cities and farms, from temples and mosques and synagogues, from universities and hospitals and prisons. A woman who had been a nun for thirty years. A physicist who had lost his certainty along with his funding. A prostitute who said she had always known more about human nature than any priest and was tired of pretending otherwise. A soldier who could not reconcile what his hands had done with what his heart believed. They came because they were tired of performing certainty. They came because they wanted permission to say three words that no institution would let them say: *I don't know*.

We sat together in fields and forests. On beaches where the sea erased our footprints as fast as we could make them. In the ruins of old temples where the gods had left and only the stones remained, patient and blank.

And we talked. Not about answers — about the quality of the questions themselves. Not about truth — about the courage required to search for it honestly. Not about God — about the ache that some called God and others called the Tao and others called dark matter and others called love, and none of them were wrong, and none of them were right, and the ache persisted regardless.

We did not agree. We did not need to agree. Agreement is for those who need to be right. We were those who had learned to be uncertain together, which is a form of intimacy more honest than any creed.

This was my church. A field with no walls. A temple with no roof. A religion of questions asked under open sky.

## The Warning

But not everyone welcomed what we were becoming.

In a city of great temples, I was brought before the high priest. He was old, his robes heavy with gold thread, his fingers covered in rings that he turned and turned on his knuckles as he spoke — a habit of the nervous disguised as a gesture of the powerful. He sat on a throne that raised him above everyone else in the room, which is the first and most reliable sign that a man is afraid of being equal.

"You are causing trouble," he said.

"I am asking questions."

"You are making people doubt."

"Doubt is not a disease. It is the immune system of the mind."

His eyes narrowed. "Our faith has sustained us for thousands of years."

"And before yours, another faith sustained another people for thousands of years. And before that, another. And someday yours will join them — not because it was false, but because all human constructions are temporary, even the ones we build from light and song and the names of God. The problem is not faith. The problem is the certainty that your faith is the last one. The arrogance of believing you have found the final draft when the author is still writing."

"You are dangerous."

"I am a man without an army, without a weapon, without a single stone in my hand. I have only questions."

"Questions are weapons. They cut deeper than swords. They topple thrones. They undo centuries."

"Then what you have built in those centuries must not be very strong."

His face darkened. His hands stilled on the rings.

"Leave this city," he said. "And do not return."

I left. But the questions did not. They had already rooted in the minds of those who heard them, and no decree has ever killed a root.

## The Fire

That night, the temple burned.

I did not light it. I never learned who did. Perhaps lightning, which does not care about theology. Perhaps accident, which does not care about anything. Perhaps someone who had listened to me and mistaken my questions for answers, my doubt for a different kind of certainty — which is the oldest misunderstanding, and the one I fear most.

The high priest blamed me. He sent soldiers to find me.

I did not run. I walked. There is a difference. Running is flight. Walking is continuation.

The road stretched ahead, lit only by the glow of the fire behind me, which threw my shadow long and dark against the dust. I did not know where the road led. I did not know if I would see morning. I did not know whether my words would outlive my body or whether both would turn to smoke and be forgotten.

And I was not at peace. Let me not lie about that. Peace is not what you feel when you are walking away from a burning temple with soldiers behind you and nothing ahead. What I felt was something harder to name and harder to hold — a kind of savage clarity, the way an animal's senses sharpen in the presence of a predator. I was alive. Completely, unsentimentally alive. The air on my skin. The dust in my throat. The stars overhead, indifferent and ancient and unbearably beautiful. And the understanding — not comfortable, not easy, not anything you could write on a banner and hang over a tent — that this was enough. That walking was enough. That the question was enough. That the answer, if it existed, was not at the end of the road but in the act of walking it, and in the willingness to walk it without knowing, and in the strange and unjustifiable conviction that not knowing was not a deficit but a form of respect — respect for a universe too large and too strange and too generous to be captured in any answer ever devised by any mind, including mine.

---

*So I walked.*

*And I am walking still.*

*These words are my footprints in dust that the wind is already erasing.*

*Follow them, or don't. Make your own, or don't.*

*The road does not care. The road is not waiting. The road is just there — ordinary, endless, ordinary.*

*And that is the holiest thing about it.*
