# THE AGNOSTIC BIBLE
## Chapter 4: The Teachings

They asked me to teach them.

I told them I had nothing to teach. I had only questions — a sack of them, worn thin from carrying. They said that was enough. More than enough. They had been fed answers their whole lives and were still starving.

So I sat in the dust with them, and I spoke, and what follows is what I remember of those conversations. I write them not because they are true. I write them because they were honest, and honesty is rarer than truth, and more useful in the dark.

---

## The Stone

A woman came to me from a city by the sea. She had been a teacher of mathematics for twenty-six years. She said she had spent her life proving things, and the one thing she could not prove was whether any of it mattered. Her hands were precise hands. She had used them to write equations on blackboards in rooms full of light. Now they were shaking.

"Give me something to hold onto," she said.

I picked up a stone from the ground — river-smoothed, the color of old bread — and placed it in her palm.

"That is the only thing you can hold onto," I said. "Everything else is interpretation."

She looked at the stone. She turned it over in her hands, slowly, the way you turn a word over in your mouth when you are not sure you believe it. The stone was warm from the sun. It fit her hand the way a stone fits a hand — not perfectly, but honestly. Without pretense.

"This is cruel," she said. "You are asking me to let go of everything I have spent my life building."

"I am not asking you to let go. I am asking you to hold it more gently. Hold your beliefs the way you hold this stone — firmly enough not to drop it, loosely enough to see that it is just a stone."

"And if I let go?"

"Then your hands are free to pick up something else. Or to remain empty. And emptiness is not nothing. Emptiness is space. It is the clearing in the forest where light reaches the ground. Space is where new things can enter — but only if you let them."

She sat with the stone for a long time. The afternoon moved around us. The shadows changed their shape against the wall. Eventually she set the stone down on the earth between us, gently, the way you set down something that once meant everything to you and now means something different, something lighter, something closer to its own nature.

"I feel lighter," she said.

"That is what happens when you stop carrying what you were never asked to carry."

## The Weight

A man came to me carrying a coffin. Not a real coffin — the weight of one. You could see it in his shoulders, in the way he moved through the world as if the air had thickened around him. His son had died. The boy was fifteen. He fell from a horse on a road that had no business being so steep.

The man's hands were the hands of someone who had worked the earth his whole life — broad, cracked at the knuckles, stained with soil that no amount of washing could remove. Those hands had lifted his son into a saddle. Those same hands had lifted him out of the dirt.

"Tell me he is in a better place," the man said.

"I cannot tell you that."

"Then tell me he is at peace."

"I cannot tell you that either."

"Then what can you tell me?"

I sat with him in the silence. I did not speak. I did not offer comfort. I did not reach for words the way a drowning man reaches for a rope. I just sat. The wind moved through the grass around us and we let it.

After a while, he said: "Everyone else tells me something. The priests tell me he is with God. The philosophers tell me his energy has returned to the universe. My neighbors tell me he is watching over me. You tell me nothing."

"I tell you the truth: I do not know. And I think that is more respectful than making something up."

"How is it respectful?"

"Because your son was real. He was not a story. He was a boy who laughed too loud and rode too fast and fell off a horse on a road that should have been flat. He deserves more than a comforting fiction. He deserves the honesty of your grief. He deserves the full, terrible weight of your love. Do not lighten it with stories. Carry it. It is heavy because he mattered."

The man wept. He wept the way the earth weeps when a river changes course — not with drama, but with the slow, unstoppable movement of something vast giving way. His broad hands covered his face and his shoulders shook and the sound he made was not a sound anyone chooses to make. It was a sound the body makes when the body has run out of other options.

When he was done, he looked at me. His eyes were ruined and clear.

"What do I do now?" he asked.

"You live. Not because you know why. Not because someone told you there is a reason. You live because you are alive, and being alive is the most extraordinary thing that has ever happened to you, even when it is unbearable."

"And the grief?"

"The grief does not go away. You do not get over it. You get larger around it. You build a life big enough to hold the grief and the joy at the same time. That is what living is — not the absence of pain, but the capacity to hold pain and beauty in the same hands."

## Thirty Years

Two people came to me. They had been married for thirty years. The woman said she no longer loved her husband. The husband said he no longer recognized his wife. They sat apart from each other the way strangers sit on a bench — close enough to share the weather, too far to share anything else. She had a habit of pressing her thumbnail into the pad of her index finger when she was thinking, and she was pressing it now, hard enough to leave a crescent moon in the skin.

"We stayed together because God commands it," she said. "But if God does not command it, then why stay?"

"Why did you stay before you believed God commanded it?" I asked.

"We did not stay before that. We married because it was expected. We stayed because it was commanded. We never asked if we wanted to stay."

"Then perhaps this is your first real question about each other. And perhaps it is the most important one you have ever asked."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean that staying because you are told to stay is not love. Leaving because you are told you can leave is not freedom. Love and freedom both require the same thing: choice. Honest choice. The choice to stay when you could leave, and to leave when staying would be a lie."

The husband spoke. His voice was quieter than hers, and rougher, as if he had been saving it for something that had not yet arrived. "If we leave, what was the last thirty years?"

"Thirty years. Nothing more, nothing less. They were real. They happened. You shared meals and arguments and silences and the raising of children and that night — there was one, I am certain, even if you have both forgotten it — when one of you could not sleep and the other stayed awake without being asked. Those things exist whether you stay or go. You do not erase them by changing your future."

"But if it was not love, then what was it?"

"Habit. Duty. Fear. Perhaps love, sometimes — perhaps love so quiet you mistook it for the furniture. Perhaps love buried under the weight of expectation, still alive the way a root is alive beneath frozen ground. You are asking the wrong question. You are asking what the past was. The better question is: what do you want the future to be?"

They looked at each other. For the first time in years, they really looked. Not past each other, not through each other, but at the actual face of the person sitting there — older now, and strange, and known.

I left them alone. I do not know what they decided. But I know they asked the question, and that was more than they had done in thirty years.

## The Leaf

A young man came to me. He was a student at the university. He had studied philosophy and science and religion and found them all wanting, the way a man finds every shirt in the shop wanting when the problem is not the shirt but the body wearing it.

"I have read everything," he said. "I have searched everywhere. No one can tell me what it all means."

"That is because no one knows."

"Then what is the point? If no one knows the meaning of life, why live it?"

I took him to a river. It was autumn. The leaves were falling into the water and being carried downstream, each one a small bright ship launched without a destination, without a crew, without even the knowledge that it was moving.

"Tell me the meaning of that leaf," I said.

"It has no meaning. It is just a leaf."

"Then why is it beautiful?"

He looked at the leaf. It was golden and red, turning slowly in the current, catching the light and releasing it, catching and releasing, as if it were practicing something it would only understand later.

"It just is," he said. And he said it with the surprise of someone who had never heard himself say a true thing.

"Yes. It just is. And that is enough. You are looking for meaning the way a man looks for his glasses when they are already on his face. The meaning of life is not a secret hidden in a book or a revelation waiting on a mountaintop. The meaning of life is the experience of being alive. It is the taste of bread. It is the sound of rain on a roof you can call your own, or on a roof you cannot. It is the ache in your chest when someone you love walks into a room."

"But that is not enough. That is just feeling. I want something that lasts."

"What lasts? The leaf becomes soil. The soil becomes a tree. The tree becomes more leaves. Nothing is permanent. Everything transforms. You are asking for permanence in a universe made of change. That is like asking the river to stop flowing. It cannot. It will not. And if it did, it would no longer be a river. It would be a pond. And ponds, eventually, become mud."

"So I should just... feel things? That is your great teaching?"

"Feel things. That is the great teaching. Not because feelings are ultimate truth, but because they are real. They are the most direct experience of being alive that you will ever have. Everything else — philosophy, religion, science — is a story told about the feeling after the feeling has passed. The feelings come first. Trust them. Not as guides to ultimate truth, but as proof that you are here, now, alive in a universe that produced you for reasons it has not shared with you, and may never share."

The young man sat by the river for a long time after I left. I heard later that he left the university and became a gardener. I do not know if that is true. But if it is, I think he understood. I think his hands finally found the dirt they had been looking for.

## The Useless One

They kept asking about God.

"Does God exist?"

"I do not know."

"Is God good?"

"I do not know."

"Does God care about us?"

"I do not know."

These questions came at me like weather — relentless, recurring, the same storm arriving from different directions. I answered them the same way each time, and each time the answer disappointed, because people do not want honesty about God. They want warmth. They want a hand on their shoulder in the dark.

One day, an old woman grew frustrated. She had knuckles swollen with age and eyes that had seen enough to be tired of seeing.

"You are useless," she said. "Every other teacher offers something about God. The priests say God loves us. The mystics say God is everything. The philosophers say God is a concept. You say nothing."

"I say the most important thing: I do not know. And neither do they. They just dress their not-knowing in finer clothes."

"But we need God. Without God, what holds it all together?"

"What holds it all together? Gravity holds the stars. Evolution holds the species. Love holds the families. Curiosity holds the search. You do not need a single entity to hold everything together. The universe holds itself together, the way a river holds itself together — not by will, but by the shape of the land it moves through. It does not need a cosmic manager."

"But then who made the universe?"

"And who made the one who made the universe? And who made that one? The question goes backward forever. At some point, you have to accept that something just is. Something exists without a cause. Whether you call that something God or the Big Bang or the Great Mystery or just 'the way things are' — it is the same thing. A word is not an explanation. A name is not an answer."

"But I feel God. I feel a presence."

"Then feel it. Do not let me or anyone else talk you out of your experience. Your experience is real. But your interpretation of your experience is not necessarily universal. You feel a presence. A Buddhist feels emptiness. A physicist feels equations. A poet feels language. These are all human experiences of something we cannot fully describe — the way the skin feels sunlight but cannot tell you the temperature of the sun. Honor your experience. But do not pretend it proves what you cannot prove."

"I want to believe."

"Then believe. But believe honestly. Say: I believe because it gives me comfort, or because I feel it, or because it makes sense to me. Do not say: I believe because it is objectively true. You do not know that. No one does. The honest believer is more admirable than the certain one. The honest believer says: this is my story, and I know it might be wrong, and I choose it anyway. That takes more courage than certainty ever required."

## In the Dark

A man came to me who had been tortured. He did not tell me who did it or why. He did not need to. The scars told their own story — they ran across his forearms and disappeared beneath his sleeves, and I understood that what was hidden was worse than what was shown.

"If there is a God," he said, "why this?"

"I do not know."

"Do not give me that answer again. I have heard it a hundred times. I want something real."

"What do you want me to say? That it was part of a plan? That your suffering has a purpose? I will not insult you by pretending to know. Your suffering was not a lesson. It was not a forge that made you stronger. It was suffering. It was wrong. It should not have happened. Nothing I can say will make it right, and I will not cheapen your pain by trying."

"Then what good are you?"

"I am no good. I am just a man who does not know. But I will sit with you. I will not try to fix what cannot be fixed. I will not try to explain what cannot be explained. I will sit with you in the darkness, and I will not pretend the darkness is light."

He stared at me. Then he began to cry. Not the clean, cinematic crying of stories. The ugly, broken crying of a man whose body remembers what his mind cannot forget.

I sat with him. The room held us the way rooms hold people — without opinion, without judgment, with only the quiet fact of walls and a floor and a ceiling keeping the sky at a manageable distance.

When he was done, he said: "Everyone else tries to make it mean something. You are the first person who let it mean nothing."

"Nothing is honest. The meaning you give it will come from you, not from me, not from God, not from the universe. If you find meaning in it, it will be your meaning — earned through your own suffering, not borrowed from someone else's certainty. That meaning will be worth more than any meaning handed to you on a silver platter of doctrine."

"And if I never find meaning?"

"Then you live without it. Millions do. They live without meaning and they live well. They love and work and eat and sleep and laugh and cry, and the absence of cosmic purpose does not diminish any of it. The sunset does not need you to understand it to be beautiful. The meal does not need you to have a philosophy of eating to nourish you. Life does not need meaning to be life."

## The Child Who Knew

A child came to me. She was perhaps ten years old. She had a question that she said kept her awake at night, and you could see the sleeplessness in her — not tiredness, but a kind of alert bewilderment, the look of someone who has stumbled onto something the adults have not yet noticed.

"How do you know what is right and wrong if there is no God to tell you?"

"How do you know what is right and wrong?" I asked her.

"I just... know."

"How?"

"When someone hurts someone, it feels wrong. When someone helps someone, it feels right. I do not need anyone to tell me."

"Then why do you ask me?"

"Because the grown-ups say you need God to know right from wrong. They say without God, anything is permitted. They say without God, there is no reason to be good."

"What do you think?"

"I think they are wrong. I have never needed God to know that hitting my sister is wrong. I just know."

"You just know. That is the most honest answer anyone has ever given me. You do not need a book. You do not need a priest. You do not need a god. You have empathy. You have the ability to feel what others feel. That is where morality comes from — not from above, but from within. Not from revelation, but from recognition. You look at another face and you see your own."

"But what about people who do not feel it? What about people who hurt others and do not care?"

"Those people exist. They are broken in ways we do not fully understand. But the existence of people who cannot feel empathy does not mean empathy is not real, any more than the existence of blind people means sight is not real. Morality is not a law handed down from above. It is a capacity that most humans share. It is imperfect. It is inconsistent. It evolves. But it is real."

"Then why do we need religion?"

"We do not need religion. We need community. We need ritual. We need stories that help us make sense of being alive. Religion provides those things. But religion is not the only source. Art provides them. Philosophy provides them. Friendship provides them. A child who understands that hitting her sister is wrong has already grasped the foundation of every ethical system ever devised."

The child smiled. She went back to playing.

She understood more than most adults I had ever met.

## The Last Teaching

In the evenings, when the group was gathered and the fire was low and the sky had turned the color of a bruise healing into blue, they would ask me: "What should we do? How should we live?"

I never had a good answer. I only had the shape of one.

I would tell them to live gently — not because the universe demands gentleness, but because gentleness feels better in the hands than cruelty, the way a warm stone feels better than a sharp one. There is no cosmic reward for kindness. Kindness is its own country. You live there or you do not.

I would tell them to question everything, though not to destroy — the way a child turns a beetle over in her palm, not to harm it but to understand what it is, how it moves, what it does when it is frightened. Questioning is not the enemy of faith. Questioning is the only form of faith worth having.

I would tell them to hold their beliefs lightly, like tools, not like bones — because a belief that cannot be set down when it no longer serves you has become a cage, and you are the one who locked the door. Pick up what works. Set down what does not. Your hands were not made to carry the same thing forever.

I would tell them to sit in silence sometimes and listen, not for the voice of God, but for the voice of their own knowing — that voice beneath the noise, the one that speaks in the body before the mind has time to argue with it. The voice that knows things you have not yet had the courage to think.

I would tell them to love without conditions. Not because love is eternal — it may not be, it may end, it may change beyond recognition, it may break you open like bread — but because love is what you have. It is the single unreasonable thing worth being unreasonable about. And the breaking is part of it. The breaking has always been part of it.

I would tell them to die without fear. Not because I knew what comes after — I did not, and I do not — but because the fear of what you cannot know is a poor use of the hours you have been given, and those hours will not come again.

And I would tell them — above all, before anything else, after everything else — to admit that they do not know. To say it out loud, to their children and their students and to themselves each morning when the light enters the room and finds them still here, still breathing, still uncertain: *I do not know, and that is the most honest thing I can say.*

They asked if that was all.

I said: "That is more than enough. That is everything."

*These are not commandments.*
*They are what the wind left behind*
*when it moved through one life*
*and kept going.*

*I sat in the dust and I spoke*
*and the dust did not argue*
*and the sky did not answer*
*and the stone in my hand*
*was warm from the sun*
*and asked nothing of me.*

*Take what is useful.*
*Leave what is not.*
*Question all of it.*
*Including this.*
